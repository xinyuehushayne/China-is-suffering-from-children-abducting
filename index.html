Images of those who are looking for their family Reasons Better conditions More focus required

       It is for ten years that “Go Back Home”, the largest missing children website in China, has been created. It is 70,000 individuals who have registered on the site, which suggests that one per 19,000 individuals in China do not know who their biological parents are. According to the average family size in China, when talking about missing children and those who do not know their original home, more than 4 million individuals are involved. Notably, this figure is only about the registered ones.

       The past ten years have witnessed the establishment and development of the national DNA database against abduction. Furthermore, the Ministry of Public Security’s “Emergency Release Platform for Missing Children’s Information” has been released. At the legal aspect, human traffickers are possibly sentenced to death, which seems to provide a little bit of hope for families to find missing children.

       Is it the case?

Profile



       It might be surprised for us to notice that among missing kids and people who are looking for their missing kids, there are more the female than the male.

       Plus, most of the missing kids are separated from their family when they are less than one year old. As a result, they are unlikely to be able to recall anything about their family.

       For most missing children, they are so young when being missing and the out-of-home period is so long that it is a root-seeking obsession to reunite with their biological parents. Nevertheless, they know that it may be impossible for them to reunite.

       Zhang Baoyan, an operator of "Back to Home", mentioned a cruel reality in the interview: the majority of missing children have no chance to return to their original family.

       Based on the thesis, we can reach a consensus that there are some regions where child abducting ratio is particularly high. Henan is recognized as one of the areas with the highest incidence of abducting and buying kids. In addition to Henan, the overall situation shows that children abducting is also frequent in Yunnan, Sichuan, Guangxi, Guizhou, and Xinjiang. According to the statistics on the relevant cases in Shandong Province between 2009 and 2014 from Wang Xizhang, a researcher, the abducted children from Yunnan, Sichuan, Guangxi, and Guizhou made up 72.6% of the total known cases. In terms of the six provinces where most abducted children are received, they are Guangdong, Zhejiang, Jiangsu, Fujian, Shandong, and Anhui. From the data I sampled, the same characteristics are also presented.


Nearly half of the children’s loss is not caused by abduction



       In the past, abductors were considered the primary culprit of out-of-home children. But in fact, up to 47.2% of the children in the sample are abandoned or sent out for adoption by their parents. Among them, the proportion of adoption is 38.3%.

       In addition to the high-frequency words such as "too young" "infant" and "unclear", words like "one-child policy" "over-birth" and "adoption", related to one another, are eye-catching as well.

       Wang Xizhang’s statistics in recent years demonstrate that as high as 72% of out-of-home children are sold by relatives, while those abducted in a violent way only account for 1%. There are diverse possible reasons for parents to sell their children: rural families violating the one-child policy unable to afford the fine, those unmarried mothers giving birth to children due to the absence of contraceptives without capabilities of raising them, wanting their children to be raised by other people with a better living condition, etc.

       Chen Baifeng and Dong Leiming’s research demonstrates that in some poverty-stricken areas, such as Shanxi Yinzhou, Sichuan Liangshan, Guangxi Yulin, Yunnan Honghe, Wenshan, and Zhaotong, some people make a living in abducting children. There is even a saying that “If you want to be rich, you must have a full belly." Moreover, there are also some “abduction villages”, where a half or even nearly 80% of the villagers have ever sold their children.

Better Conditions

       In fact, anti-abducting policies have been issued for a long time. In 2009, the national DNA database was established. In May 2016, the Criminal Investigation Bureau of the Ministry of Public Security created a national platform, Reunion - the Emergency Platform for Missing Children's Information, to fight against abducting children through the Internet. In December 2016, the Interpretation of the Supreme People's Court on Several Issues Concerning the Specific Application of Laws in the Trial of Crimes of Trafficking in Women and Children was promulgated, clearly stating that deception, inducement and other means used to make infants or/and young children removed from guardians or caregivers are regarded as criminal acts. The clauses relevant to "Stealing infants and young children" are in Article 2-4, paragraph 1 (6). The judicial interpretation will take effect since January 1, 2017. According to Article 245 of the Criminal Law of China, those who abduct or sell a woman or a child shall be sentenced to fixed-term imprisonment of no less than five years and no more than ten years and fined. Those who steal children for the purpose of selling shall be sentenced to fixed-term imprisonment of no less than ten years or the life imprisonment, and fined or confiscated property. If the circumstances are particularly severe, the death penalty shall be imposed, and the criminal’s property shall be confiscated, which means that abductors can even be sentenced to death. In addition, the implementation of the second-child policy has also improved the relevant conditions. Chen Shiqu, the director of the Criminal Investigation Bureau of the Ministry of Criminal Investigation, said in an interview with Zhongxin.com that it is found that some people wanted to buy a child due to the one-child policy. For those who want to have more than one child, they tend to buy children.

        Better conditions are suggested in the number of missing people: since 1932, the number of missing people increased gradually, with a small soar between 1959 and 1960, reached its peak around 1990, and has steadily decreased since then. Although the figure goes up in recent years, it is much better than before. The successful cases in each year also indicate a positive trend year by year that we are getting closer and closer to the beautiful vision of “no abduction in China”.



More Efforts Required



        Although the situations have improved and the number of missing children is decreasing continuously, we still have to admit that in the face of a huge base, the number of successful cases seems to be a drop in the bucket – the overall recovery ratio is only 4.3%, which means that only 4 of 100 missing children reunite with their original family. Even for those children reuniting with parents, it is likely to take 20 or 30 years to return home, which is a cruel fact.

        We have a tough long way to go. After analyzing 2,801 successful cases, it is estimated that even without one case of missing children in 2018, there is a 91% chance that all missing children cannot find their way home until 2338, 321 years later. Zhang Baoyan, the founder of the "Baby Go Home" website, once said, "we will not retire unless all parents find their missing children." She is 55 years old now.
